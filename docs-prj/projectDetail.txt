</tr><tr><td width="30%" bgcolor="5"><font size="10" color="#000000"><b>Project goals</b></font></td><td>Research - Impact of institutional audits, particularly on institutional capacity to manage appropriate forms of change, as well as other possible effects<br /><br />Accountability, improvement and development of higher education institutions’ quality management systems. This is done through the quality audit of the effectiveness of the arrangements for the management of quality in the three core functions of teaching and learning, research and community engagement of all public providers and the qualifying private providers of higher education.<br /></td>
</tr><tr><td width="30%" bgcolor="5"><font size="10" color="#000000"><b>Design and methodology</b></font></td><td>While the main focus of the 2006 study was on the effectiveness and efficiency of the HEQC approach, methodology and processes, and while this will continue to be important in the 2007 study, the importance of the relationship between audit and institutional change and development will be given additional attention in 2007. <br /><br />1.	The study will begin to track the ways in which individual learning affects, influences and shapes institutional learning both in those institutions audited in 2005 and 2007 as well as in the still to-be-audited institutions of panel members.<br /><br />2.	The study will attempt to develop deeper understandings of how audit processes assist in institutional development and change and to identify facilitating factors, including how this development becomes organizational learning. <br />3.	The inclusion of two institutions that participated in the 2006 study will provide the HEQC will opportunities to track the ways in which recommendations in Audit Reports have been responded to and taken up.<br />4.	The study will provide the HEQC with the opportunity to investigate the perceptions, experiences and activities within institutions during audit preparation, immediately after the conduct of the audit visit and during the development and implementation of the Improvement Plan.<br />5.	The study will contribute to the HEQC impact studies on institutional audit.<br />Objectives<br />1.	To gather evidence of the ways in which the different stages of institutional audits affect HEIs. Specifically, the HEQC would like<br />a.	To gather evidence on the ways in which individuals and working groups are briefed about audit and its purposes<br />b.	To gather evidence about the ways in which these individuals and working groups respond to the briefings and then to the work required in developing the Self-Evaluation Report<br />c.	To gather evidence about processes used during audit preparation and how these affect individual and institutional development<br />d.	To gather evidence about how the Audit Report is disseminated and responded to within institutions<br />e.	To gather evidence about how the recommendations in the Audit Report are used in the development of the Self-Improvement Plan<br />f.	To gather evidence as to changes and the introduction of new policies and practices at all the stages of audit.<br />2.	To gather evidence of the effect that the audit experience had on members of the audit panel and whether there has been any transference of such experience to their own institutions.<br />3.	To test an evaluative post-audit instrument to help the HEQC standardize its gathering of information at the end of each site visit.<br />4.	To identify improvement initiatives in each of the institutions visited, the developments of which can be tracked in further research studies.<br />Methodology<br />1.	Refinement of the heuristic tool based on the Framework for institutional audits and used in the 2006 study. This tool was used in the formulation of questions and / or in the identification of high-level topics to be included in interviews. Given the broader timeline of the 2007 project, this tool will need to be further developed in certain areas and in line with additional specific foci. <br />2.	Documentary work: Institutional Profiles, Self-Evaluation Portfolios, Audit Reports, HEIs own evaluations of the audit experience and, where possible, HEIs own survey instruments and methodologies need to be analysed in order to identify instances of policy, procedures or structural developments in the institutions.<br />3.	Face to face interviews with individuals and focus groups: interviews need to cover the different layers of the institution from management to the academic units in order to have both ‘’expert’’ and ‘’non-expert’’ information.<br />4.	Testing of the newly-developed consolidated post-audit questionnaire used at various institutions as part of the debriefing process, and its refinement. <br />5.	Thirty telephonic interviews with audit panel members, chairs and report writers will be conducted. Interviews will be 20 - 30 minutes each.<br /></td>

</tr><tr><td width="30%" bgcolor="5"><font size="10" color="#000000"><b>Deliverables and planned outputs</b></font></td><td>1.	As with the 2006 study, a small team will undertake the research under a research leader. <br />2.	A primary researcher will be appointed to each institution included in the study. This person will be responsible for preparation (reading documents, specifying questions, setting up appointments) and will undertake the majority of interviews as well as write up the case study report. Where appropriate, a further researcher will accompany her for senior staff interviews. Primary researchers will also be responsible for conducting a portion of the telephonic interviews with auditors, chairpersons and report writers.<br />3.	Data collection will be structured and adjusted as necessary, in agreement with the HEQC.<br />4.	Presentation of findings and report-writing. Findings from work completed before June will be presented to the HEQC at the end of July. The research leader will be responsible for oversight and monitoring as well as the completion of the final consolidated research report. The report will be submitted by the end of November 2007. <br /><br />Outputs<br />•	Report on the evaluative instrument<br />•	Refined evaluative instrument<br />•	Research report<br />•	Evidence gathered including interview summaries</td>

</tr><tr><td width="30%" bgcolor="5"><font size="10" color="#000000"><b>Background and rationale</b></font></td><td>In terms of the Higher Education Act (Act No 101 of 1997), the HEQC performs the quality promotion and quality assurance functions of the Council on Higher Education, which includes accrediting programmes of higher education. The specific focus of HEQC national reviews is the accreditation of existing programmes in a selected area.  National reviews are one of the HEQC's frameworks to deal with quality in the provision of learning programmes. The Higher Education Quality Committee of the Council on Higher Education is undertaking a national review of professional and academic programmes in education focused on four different types of programmes over a three year period: M.Ed.; B.Ed.; PGCE; and ACE.  This project (Code 91) includes the M Ed Review only.</td>

</tr><tr><td width="30%" bgcolor="5"><font size="10" color="#000000"><b>Background and rationale</b></font></td><td>In March 2005, the CHE established a Task Team to investigate the past decade of regulation of South African higher education by government and other agencies, and to promote debate on conceptions of institutional autonomy, academic freedom and public accountability, in general, and in the specific context of higher education transformation.  Amid concerns and claims by some that the nature of government involvement in higher education is in danger of moving from ‘state steering’ to ‘state interference’, the CHE believed it important to undertake a sober and rigorous investigation of the issues, so giving effect to the CHE’s responsibilities independently to advise the Minister of Education, to monitor and evaluate higher education, and to contribute to higher education development.</td>
</tr><tr><td width="30%" bgcolor="5"><font size="10" color="#000000"><b>Project goals</b></font></td><td>The Task Team investigation aims to:<br />•	Stimulate research and writing<br />•	Build shared understandings of  institutional autonomy, academic freedom and public accountability, through the creation of various public forums, public discussion and debate on these important principles<br />•	Develop consensus, as far as is possible, on the nature and modes of government involvement in higher education transformation, and on the relationships between government and other regulatory bodies and HEIs.<br /><br />The Task Team aims to:<br />-	Critically analyse the nature and modes of government and other regulatory bodies’ involvement in higher education and its transformation and development<br />-	Identify and critically assess the conceptions of institutional autonomy, academic freedom and public accountability that are held by key higher education actors <br />-	Produce a report on the nature of the involvement of government and regulatory bodies in higher education, on various conceptions of institutional autonomy, academic freedom and public accountability and on their efficacy in general, and with respect to higher education transformation and development.</td>
</tr><tr><td width="30%" bgcolor="5"><font size="10" color="#000000"><b>Design and methodology</b></font></td><td>Methodology:<br />In July 2005, the Task Team agreed its terms of reference and programme of work.  Five key mechanisms were adopted for the purposes of information-gathering, research and engagement, namely: 1) a commissioned overview of recent and current debates; 2) stakeholder submissions; 3) commissioned research; 4) interviews; and 5) structured fora.</td>
